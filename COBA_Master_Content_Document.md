### Sub-Page 1: Career Paths to Explore for Finance Majors

**Display**: Interactive career cards (not slideshow) - all visible at once

#### Career Path 1: Investment Banking üíº
**Culture**: Fast-paced, competitive, and demanding. Emphasis on teamwork and deal execution.
**Work-Life Balance**: Typically long hours, especially during deal closings. Limited work-life balance.
**Pay**: High base salaries and substantial bonuses. Bonuses often make up a significant portion of total compensation.

#### Career Path 2: Hedge Funds üìà
**Culture**: Varied, depending on the fund's strategy. Can range from highly collaborative to individual performance-driven.
**Work-Life Balance**: Can be demanding, especially during market fluctuations. Some funds offer more flexibility.
**Pay**: Highly variable. Performance-based compensation is a significant factor.

#### Career Path 3: Asset Management üìä
**Culture**: Diverse, depending on the type of asset management (mutual funds, pension funds, etc.). Can be more stable compared to hedge funds.
**Work-Life Balance**: Generally better than investment banking or hedge funds, but still depends on the specific role and company.
**Pay**: Competitive, with bonuses tied to performance.

#### Career Path 4: Corporate Finance üè¢
**Culture**: Varies widely based on the industry and company. Generally more stable and less intense than investment banking.
**Work-Life Balance**: Generally better work-life balance compared to investment banking or private equity.
**Pay**: Competitive base salaries with potential for performance-based bonuses.

#### Career Path 5: Insurance üõ°Ô∏è
**Culture**: Tends to be more risk-averse and stable. Emphasis on actuarial and risk management.
**Work-Life Balance**: Can vary but is often more predictable and stable compared to some other finance sectors.
**Pay**: Competitive, with variations depending on roles such as underwriting, actuarial, or management.

### Sub-Page 2: What Jobs Can I Get in Data Analytics?

**Display**: Career cards (NOT slideshow) - all 8 careers visible at once

#### Career 1: Financial Analyst üí∞
**Purpose**: Review data to generate finance and market reports. Make recommendations for business growth in complex global economy. Highlight trends and patterns impacting business strategies.

**Skills**: Recommended degree in statistics, computer science, business administration, or related field. Understand data mining, analysis, processing, and visualization. Utilizes marketing and predictive analytics. Willing to constantly learn about changes in state, federal, and international communities.

#### Career 2: Business Intelligence Analyst üìä
**Purpose**: Generate financial statements that accurately reflect businesses' position. Work in banking, advising, insurance companies, and pension funds. Assess quality of receivable accounts, payable accounts, investments, and spending.

**Skills**: Must communicate complex financial concepts to encourage informed business decisions. Utilize visualization software to inform stakeholders on investments, spending, performance, and bonds.

#### Career 3: Credit Analyst üí≥
**Purpose**: Evaluate financial reliability of individuals or companies. Collect financial details from clients including income and previous repayment records to gauge capacity to meet financial commitments.

**Skills**: Focus on capacity, collateral, covenants, and character. Requires bachelor's degree in finance, accounting, or related field. Extensive knowledge of financial analysis and credit risk assessment.

#### Career 4: Marketing Analyst üìà
**Purpose**: Perform complex tasks to help companies gauge competitive positions. Understand consumers' wants and needs and reflect trends in business practices. Advise companies on how to best position themselves in the market.

**Skills**: Recommended degrees in statistics, mathematics, marketing, social sciences, or similar fields. Proficient with computer programs such as Microsoft Excel, Access, and PowerPoint. Recommended knowledge of analytical software including SAS, SPSS, and Google Analytics.

#### Career 5: Data Visualization Analyst üìâ
**Purpose**: Create visual representations of data intended for data professionals and organizational leaders. Convey extensive information, especially big data, in formats that are comprehensible and valuable for spotting patterns and irregularities.

**Skills**: Proficiency in data visualization enables conveying data to team using graphs and charts. Essential to acquire technical skills including Excel, programming languages like R or Python, statistical knowledge, and proficiency in SQL.

#### Career 6: Fraud Analyst üîç
**Purpose**: Hold pivotal position in protecting organizations against financial losses from fraudulent activities. Duties encompass identification and examination of suspicious transactions, scrutiny of data to identify fraud patterns, and creates strategies to prevent fraud.

**Skills**: Proficiency in data visualization enables conveying data to team using graphs and charts. Essential to acquire technical skills including Excel, programming languages like R or Python, statistical knowledge, and proficiency in SQL.

#### Career 7: Real Estate Analyst üè†
**Purpose**: Analyze real estate market trends, property values, and investment opportunities. Provide data-driven insights for real estate investment decisions.

**Skills**: Strong analytical abilities, financial modeling, market research skills, and proficiency in data analysis tools and software.

#### Career 8: Risk Analyst ‚ö†Ô∏è
**Purpose**: Assess company's investment portfolios and evaluate risk associated with decisions related to those investments. Employ analytical abilities to anticipate potential losses and provide suggestions for decreasing risk by diversification, currency exchanges, and various investment tactics.

**Skills**: Include strong analytical abilities, enabling evaluation of data, pattern recognition, and risk assessment for various investments and scenarios. Solid grasp of financial concepts such as risk management, portfolio analysis, and financial modeling is vital. Proficiency in mathematical and statistical methods is crucial for quantifying and measuring risks accurately.

### Sub-Page 7: How To Dress Professionally

**Title**: "A Quick Guide to Professional Dress for Men and Women"
**Presented by**: The Career Services Ambassadors (CSA)

**Display**: Visual examples with text descriptions

#### Section 1: Business Professional Attire

**For Women** (For any first meeting, business presentation, and job interview):
- Black skirt or pant suit, other neutral colors (dark gray, navy, brown) okay as long as top and bottom are matching
- Skirt suits recommended; skirts should be a little below the knee and never shorter than above the knee
- Pressed, collared button-down shirt
- Nude (skin-tone) or dark-colored panty-hose and closed-toe heels
- Dress shoes (pumps, flats, heels) ‚Äì No platform or >2.5 inch heels
- Make-up should be minimal and conservative (daytime makeup)
- No visible tattoos; pale color nail polish; no perfume or body sprays
- Long hair should be pulled back away from face
- **Tip**: Keep hair and jewelry simple (i.e. studs for earrings); remove all other jewelry and piercings (i.e. nose rings, etc.)

**Visual**: [4 professional women in business suits - navy, black, gray]

**For Men** (For any first meeting, business presentation, and job interview):
- Full suit with matching jacket and pants with black belt
- Pressed, collared shirt with plain (solid, stripe, or simple pattern) tie
- Dark leather dress shoes and dark dress socks
- The bottom of the tie should just barely cover the entire belt buckle; a tie should never be too long or too short
- No cologne, visible tattoos or piercings
- Light briefcase or portfolio case
- **Tip**: A three-piece suit is acceptable for an interview, but a suit with mismatched pants and jacket is not recommended.

**Visual**: [Professional men in full business suits]

---

#### Section 2: Business Casual Attire

**For Men**:
- Pressed, collared, long-sleeved shirt with tie
- Dress slacks, socks, belt, and shoes
- Shirts should always be tucked in
- Jeans UNACCEPTABLE
- Closed-toe shoes

**For Women**:
- Dress pants, skirt (at or below knee), or dress okay
- Fitted blouses and sweaters/cardigans, scarves okay
- Closed-toe shoes (pumps/heels <2.5 inches)
- **Avoid**: Blingy/shiny/lacey fabric, sequins, khaki, skinny and capri pants, shorts, tight skirts, skirts shorter than knee length, low-cut and sleeveless blouses/dress, large jewelry, bulky purse, sandals, wedges, and heels > 2.5 inches.
- **Tip**: Business casual = business professional, with ONE of the articles substituted. (Ex: Full suit with a shell top for women or a full suit minus the jacket or the tie for men.)

**Visual**: [4 people in business casual attire]
